Section 1 of 3

Introduction

Belize, Toledo, and Punta Gorda

A short, plain-language orientation to where you are going and how to be a respectful guest. Everything here comes from the study documents and from cited background reading.

Why you are here

As part of the Global Health Institute Summer Program at MEZCOPH, University of Arizona, two MPH students travel to Punta Gorda from mid-July to mid-August to assist Dr. Mario Morales with the THRIVE-Belize feasibility study. You will stay at Hillside Health Care as part of an ongoing collaboration; at Hillside, Dr. Anja Giphart supports the students.

THRIVE-Belize is a proposed school life-skills programme for students at Toledo Community College (TCC). Right now there is no programme to teach - the study asks the school and community whether such a programme would be welcome, fitting, and workable.

Source: prompt project brief; Protocol, Roles and Responsibilities; Supplement D, Section 1.

Where you are

Simplified orientation drawing, not to scale. Belize faces the Caribbean Sea, with Mexico to the north and Guatemala to the west and south. The Toledo District is the south; Punta Gorda is its main town. Drawn for this app from standard geography described in the cited sources.

  • Belize is a small country on the Caribbean coast of Central America. It was a British colony (British Honduras) and became independent in 1981. English is its official language.
  • The Toledo District is the southern, rural region - and the most underserved part of the country for health and social services.
  • Toledo Community College (TCC) is a co-educational secondary school in Punta Gorda Town, enrolling about 700 students in Forms 1-4 (ages 12-18), drawn from Punta Gorda and surrounding Maya and Garifuna villages.

Source: Moberg 2020, "Belize," Oxford Handbook of Central American History (p. 637); Supplement G, Section 8.1; Protocol, Section 2.1.

Punta Gorda in a nutshell

  • Punta Gorda ("PG") town is small and very mixed - Garifuna, Mestizo, Maya (Q'eqchi' and Mopan), Creole, East Indian and others live side by side. PG is the Garifuna cultural center of southern Belize. The villages around it are mostly Maya.
  • It is religious and conservative, and most schools are church-run (Roman Catholic most of all). Topics like sexual and reproductive health are genuinely sensitive; expect strong feelings when those topics come up.
  • TCC's students are predominantly Maya (Q'eqchi' and Mopan) and Mestizo, with smaller numbers of Garifuna, Creole, East Indian and Mennonite students.

Source: Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 3 (3.3); Protocol, Section 2.1.

People and languages

English is the language of instruction, but it is rarely the only language in the room:

  • Kriol is the everyday lingua franca; Spanish is common (especially Mestizo families); Q'eqchi' and Mopan Maya are widely spoken - in Toledo, more people speak a Mayan language than speak English. Garifuna is also spoken.
  • You will work in English, Spanish, and informally Kriol. You have no interpreters. Many older or rural Maya - especially women and elders - have limited English or Spanish.
  • If someone cannot take part in a language you share, do not improvise a translation and do not push. Thank them warmly and explain you are not able to include everyone this time. Note it - this is a known limit of the study, not a personal failure.
  • Keep your own language plain and slow. Check understanding by asking the person to say it back in their own words - not "Do you understand?"
A note on the numbers. Across Belize, the population is roughly Mestizo, Creole, Maya, Garifuna and others. At TCC specifically, the recruitment plan estimates about 45% Maya, 35% Mestizo, 15% Garifuna, and 5% other. Treat all of these as estimates, never as facts about any individual.

Source: Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 3 (3.4-3.5); Protocol, Sections 2.1 and 2.7; Grant 2022 (p. 284); Moberg 2020 (p. 645).

The one idea to hold: cultural humility

Cultural humility is a posture of respect and self-awareness - not a checklist of facts about "other cultures."

You are guests gathering people's opinions about a proposed school programme. Stay curious, stay humble, and let people be the experts on their own community. If someone objects on religious or moral grounds, thank them and record the view - do not debate or try to persuade. Faith is part of the answer, not the enemy: churches run most schools here, and many faith leaders care about the same young people you do.

Source: Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 3 (3.2, 3.6).

Working respectfully with each community

These are general tendencies, not facts about any individual. Younger, town-based, more-schooled people are usually more open. Use them as starting points and follow each person's lead.

Maya (Q'eqchi' and Mopan)

The largest group in the district, mostly rural. Respect for elders runs deep; a silence or indirect answer is often politeness or thinking, not refusal - give time. SRH is especially private. If you go into a Maya village, ask permission first through the alcalde and village council, not only the school. Offer separate women's and men's groups, each led by a same-sex team member, for anything touching SRH.

Garifuna

PG's anchor community; almost all speak English/Kriol, so language is rarely a barrier. Proud, distinct culture. One firm rule: the dugu ancestral ceremony is sacred and closed - never ask to attend it or treat it as data.

Mestizo

Spanish is often the home language; offer Spanish forms and lead in Spanish. Predominantly Roman Catholic; expect conservative views on SRH.

Creole

English/Kriol; mostly Protestant. Some congregations are among the strongest opponents of sex education - keep SRH framing neutral.

East Indian

Long settled near PG, English/Kriol, well integrated - a standard respectful approach is fine.

Mennonite

A small community (Pine Hill), German-speaking and deliberately separate. They will likely decline, and that is fine: invite, never press, and do not treat their participation as needed.

Source: Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 3 (3.5).

A little more context (handled gently)

  • Extended family is central to social life, child-rearing, and financial support; Roman Catholic influence is strong among Mestizo, Maya and Garifuna families, and many high schools teach abstinence.
  • In traditional rural Maya communities, gender roles are conservative and girls' lives are often more closely watched - one reason SRH topics feel private.
  • Mental-health services are scarce across Belize, and in the rural south many people consult trusted local or traditional healers first. This is part of why clear referral pathways and cultural humility matter so much in your work.

Source: Rich 2017, "Women in Belize" (pp. 241, 243, 252); Grant 2022 (pp. 288-290). These academic chapters are cited for background and are not redistributed here.

Staying safe as a team

  • Work in pairs, in daylight, in public or known places. Tell Mario (the PI, on site) where you are and when you will check in. Keep your phone and the contact card on you. If something feels off, leave.
  • The $5 is a thank-you for time, not a lure. Mention it plainly and last; never use it to push someone who is hesitant, and be especially careful with young people and people in hardship.
  • Hearing hard things wears on you. Debrief with the team each day, share the load, and tell Mario if you are struggling.

Source: Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 3 (3.8, 3.9).

The Protocol

What the study is, and what you will collect

THRIVE-Belize Phase I is a feasibility study. It does not deliver any lessons. It asks students, teachers, parents, school leaders, and community members what they think of a proposed life-skills programme - so the team can learn whether it is acceptable, appropriate, and workable before any pilot.

The study in plain words

  • Type: a single-school, mixed-methods feasibility study at TCC. It is registered as an observational study; no curriculum is delivered in this phase.
  • It asks two questions: (1) Is the THRIVE-Belize curriculum acceptable, appropriate, and feasible to implement at TCC? (2) What adaptations are needed before a pilot?
  • How: paper surveys, plus audio-recorded focus groups and interviews, across five stakeholder groups.

Source: Protocol, Abstract and Sections 1.4, 2.9.

▸ Plain-language summary (text alternative)

The protocol describes a Phase I feasibility study at Toledo Community College in Punta Gorda, Belize. Young people in rural southern Belize face several health challenges, and schools are a promising place for health promotion, but no comprehensive life-skills programme has been tested here. Before any pilot, the team needs to know whether stakeholders see such a programme as acceptable, appropriate, and workable.

The study collects paper surveys, focus groups, and interviews from students, teachers, parents and guardians, school leaders, and community stakeholders. It measures five feasibility domains - acceptability, appropriateness, feasibility, adoption/readiness, and safety/safeguarding - using short adapted scales, and analyses the results with simple descriptive statistics and thematic analysis. Strong safeguards apply throughout: voluntary participation, the right to skip questions, clear limits to confidentiality, private data collection, and referral pathways. Pre-set "traffic-light" progression criteria will guide whether the project moves on to a pilot trial.

The daily routine

  • Surveys go into sealed envelopes labelled by date, then into a locked filing cabinet in a locked room at Hillside Health Care. Consent forms are stored in a separate drawer (PI only).

Supplement G · Safeguarding High priority

How to protect the young people you meet - and what to do if someone discloses harm or distress. Read this until it is second nature.

If someone discloses: the NSPCC 4Rs

Use the internationally recognised framework - Recognise, Respond, Report, Record. The Respond step is what you do in the moment:

RWhat you do
RecogniseNotice a disclosure or concern is happening - including indirect signs (a sudden mood change, "asking for a friend," reluctance to go home).
RespondGive full attention; let them speak at their own pace; do not interrupt. Stay calm even if you are shocked - your calm keeps them safe. Reassure and believe: "Thank you for telling me. I believe you. This is not your fault." Be honest about the limits of confidentiality and never promise secrecy: "I can't promise to keep this secret, but I will only tell people who need to know to help you." Do not interrogate, ask leading questions, or investigate.
ReportReport to the Designated Safeguarding Lead within 2 hours. The DSL triages the risk and makes referrals.
RecordDocument factually, in the participant's own words, on the Safeguarding Concern Form - using the study ID, never the name.

What NOT to do

  • Don't promise to keep it secret
  • Don't interrogate or ask leading questions
  • Don't show shock, disgust, or disbelief
  • Don't confront the alleged abuser
  • Don't gossip or tell anyone who does not need to know
  • Don't try to handle it yourself

Remember

Careful, caring listening does not create harm. What harms is silence, judgment, and broken promises. You notice and connect people to help - you do not treat.

Source: Supplement G, Sections 6.1-6.2; Staff Safety Training Guide, Module 1 (1.4).

Suicide-risk screening: the Columbia Protocol (C-SSRS)

You will not routinely screen anyone. The C-SSRS is there for the rare time someone discloses thoughts of suicide or self-harm. It is a short set of plain-language questions. Anyone may administer it - but only after completing the free official training and getting a certificate. Asking about suicide does not increase risk; it often brings relief.

▸ The six screener questions and what they trigger
  1. In the past month, have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?
  2. In the past month, have you actually had any thoughts of killing yourself?

If YES to question 2, also ask:

  1. Have you been thinking about how you might do this?
  2. Have you had these thoughts and had some intention of acting on them?
  3. Have you started to work out or worked out the details of how to kill yourself? Do you intend to carry out this plan?
  4. Have you ever done anything, started to do anything, or prepared to do anything to end your life? (If yes: was this within the past 3 months?)
Answer patternRiskAction
Yes to Q4, Q5, or Q6HighImmediate referral; do not leave the person alone; call emergency services if needed
Yes to Q2 or Q3ModerateSame-day referral to mental-health services
Yes to Q1 onlyLowerRoutine referral; provide support resources

The C-SSRS is copyright Columbia University. Always use the official screener form for the exact wording and read the cut-points off the instrument. Confirm the current official version before fieldwork.

Documentation and the law

  • Document every concern on the Safeguarding Concern Form within 2 hours: date, time, location, the participant's study ID (never their name), their words verbatim, what you observed, what you did, and the DSL notification.
  • Under Belize's Families and Children Act, suspected child abuse must be reported; the study adopts mandatory reporting as its ethical standard. The age of majority in Belize is 18.

Supplement A · The Instruments Medium priority

The surveys and guides you will administer. The main content here is shown in English only; the full document includes Spanish and provisional Maya versions.

  • 15 instruments in three parts: 6 surveys (students, teachers, parents/guardians, principal, admin/support staff, community informants); 6 in-depth interview guides; 3 focus-group guides.
  • Surveys use one 5-point scale: Strongly Disagree, Disagree, Not Sure, Agree, Strongly Agree.
  • Every question is framed prospectively - what people expect would happen if THRIVE were offered - because the curriculum has not been delivered.

How to administer (enumerator rules)

  • Use simple explanations; never pressure anyone to answer.
  • For student surveys you may read questions aloud and explain the meaning of words - but do not suggest answers.
  • Do not ask participants to share personal stories of violence, abuse, or illegal behaviour.
  • Participation is voluntary; anyone may skip questions or stop at any time without penalty. If someone discloses a safety concern, follow Supplement G.
▸ Example: the Student Survey (A1), in English

Read aloud before starting: "This is a research study. We are asking students at Toledo Community College about a program called THRIVE that may be offered in the future... You can skip any question you do not want to answer. We will not put your name on this form."

The survey has short sections matching the five domains, for example:

  • Acceptability: "I would be interested in lessons that help students handle stress and strong feelings."
  • Appropriateness: "These topics can be taught in a way that respects Belizean culture and values."
  • Feasibility: "A lesson time of about 40 minutes sounds realistic for our school day."
  • Safety/safeguarding: "Students would know where to get help if they are worried about safety or health."

These items are quoted from the English Student Survey (A1) for orientation. Always administer from the official printed form.

Supplement E · Consent and Assent Medium priority

No one takes part until they have freely said yes. For students, that means two signatures, in order.

  • Students (ages 12-17): two steps. First the parent or guardian gives written permission; then the student gives their own written assent. A student aged 18 gives their own consent.
  • Active (opt-in) consent only. Parents must affirmatively sign and return forms - there is no opt-out.
  • Forms exist for principals, admin staff, teachers, students (assent), parents (permission for their child, and for their own participation), and community stakeholders - in English, Spanish, Q'eqchi', and Mopan. If someone cannot read, a staff member reads the form aloud in their preferred language.
  • Every form explains: it is research; what will happen; that it is voluntary; that they can skip or stop; privacy protections; and the limits of confidentiality (a disclosure of serious harm must be shared to keep someone safe).
▸ What students are promised (from the assent form)
  • "It is 100% your choice. If you say no, nothing bad will happen. Your grades will not change."
  • "Your name is not on the survey. We use a code number instead."
  • "If you tell us that you are being hurt or that you want to hurt yourself, we must tell someone who can help you. This is to keep you safe."

Quoted from the English Adolescent Assent form (Form D).
